date: 2026-06-29

The task list process has been successfully implemented into TRG, it took a bit of experimentation and a touch of dis-trust to what an AI can do. My coding companion has been Antigravity, for a while now, that offers limited access to a number of LLMs for free. You can select between them and switch to continue your work if you reach the limit with one of them. One at a time, these models will be your personal coding agent to co-develop with. Personally I've mostly used Opus and Gemini models, in different flavors and spices.

Results are great, for the most part. I truly enjoy the entire "implementation plan" concept, where I get to review the AI's plan before it marches on with absolute confidence to potentially create something beautiful or absolutely destroy my code base, initiating an unbearable domino effect of decencies falling one after the other.

Researching prompt engineering for my project has helped me to create some prompts that yield valuable results which allow me to progress a lot quicker to what I would achieve without this kind of help. It's quick but still requires more than 10-15 minutes just to type my prompt, even more for complex cases. I am very familiar with my codebase, how everything comes together, thus my prompts are very targeted. I've tried wide projects before, trying to refactor too many things at once but it did not go so well. Once or twice I would spent a good few hours trying to figure out what went so horribly wrong. Context is king. The more specific and targeted the better, the wider it is the worse the result. 

I also figured out that results are heavily dependent on what you are trying to accomplish. In simple terms, the AIs are -in my experience- FAR better front-end than back-end developers.

If I have to change something in the front-end, I am always happy to let a few things for the AI to figure out, an it surprises me every single time. Results are elegant and minimal, as if it can understand what modern software should look like. I've seen the jokes that models will tend to use many emojis, and they are based on true facts, however I find that in most cases emojis are used in a beautiful and playful way rather than a cringe or a cheesy one. It can also be argued that most software look the same at this moment, but let me tell you, the standard "just works", it's nice to look at and people have used it so much that they immediately understand it. So for an app, like TRG, that aims to be used and not to be an innovative UX research project. It's a beauty!

When it comes to the back-end, it is a completely different picture. It's dark and gloomy here. Letting AI do "its thing" rarely works and I need to carefully guide it through. Don't get me wrong, it will nail things that are "textbook" but when it gets time to build the software's logic, the business logic, it does not perform. You need to be always present, review everything, take notes, draw sketches. Letting models free-roam back there is dangerous!

Having an AI coding partner is truly a dangerous beauty.
