Been a big fun of Fireship for a long time now, even before the AI days, and 10 or so days ago he posted the installment of his code report series with the title "Anthropic is starting to panic". Near the end of the video he mentions a report published by one of MIT's open source media labs called "MIT NANDA". The report is called "The GenAI Divide - State of AI in Business 2025" and it is a pretty interesting read. In the "spy" version of this blog, I want to go over a very brief overview of the report, and I'll probably do a deeper dive in the following days as I read it again and again.

Disclaimer. As the researchers mention at the end of their report, this work is not representative of every single instance of AI being implemented into business. It is interview-based, meaning that people could refuse to express their opinion, and also that those who are willing to discuss "may systematically differ from those declining participation". Additionally it would be impossible to have global coverage for every single sector there is.

The researchers start things off very strong with two statements. An estimated 30-40 billion dollars was invested for generative AI by businesses. The overwhelming majority of the organizations report no return on their investment. What comes as a bigger surprise is that 5% of the organizations interviewed report gaining millions in value. What comes as no surprise are the that the most used tools reported are ChatGPT and Copilot. Such individual, general purpose tools represent the majority of the tools used, while custom solutions focused on enterprise wide domains are not adopted. As someone who personally builds such a custom tool this is a worrying sign.

The report goes on exploring their "GenAI Divide" concept thoroughly through a number of different viewpoints. A pattern that keeps re-appearing is that successful tools and organizations that have seen a return on their generative AI investment share a common strategy. Success comes from adaptability and cooperation. In chapter five we see that happy stories are built upon system customization, context aware and applications build on instructive feedback. 

People who build one-and-done tools and organizations that try such tools, watch as their efforts progress slightly before failing to graduate from the pilot stage. Applications that are easy to demo leave a good first impression, however they typically fail to stick "due to lack of memory and customization" as the researchers state. If the new tool does not match the current workflow, does not improve it and ultimately disrupts it, that's a recipe for failure.

Both the organizations and the vendors need to share a common mindset. Organizations must "hold vendors accountable" and vendors should allow room for discussion. It's not a client relationship, it is a partnership.
