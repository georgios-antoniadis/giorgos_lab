Back on my feet. Well... sort of. It's not a switch or a button, it's a progress in it of itself, that will probably take while. Today's step is materializing the entire to-do-list thing into a complete package that will be shipped with TRG. First things first, save your progress, no need for more disasters.

Saving progress on my software projects, for as long as I can remember, means publishing the thing on GitHub, same as millions of others I can only guess. This gets me thinking. How come a software monopoly as strong as GitHub gets this little hate? Typically, you'll see people hate hard on software monopolies. Microsoft, that owns GitHub mind you, gets so much hate for products like Windows and Microsoft teams. Apple will get hate for their "closed garden" approach to iOS and MacOS. Netflix and other similar services will have people not like them so much that they will go out of their way to set-up a home-brew solution that they will then publish it for free for everyone! People are out here pitching, to investors, their new revolutionary AI SaaS every 15 minutes before they even write a single line of code, while others will spend weeks working on a side project and then open-source it out of pure frustration for the giant monopolies? The latest example that comes to mind is the case of PewDiePie's Odysseus project, although it can be argued that he is a different kind of individual all together. 

So let me finally get to the big question. How come our landing pages are not full of people publishing their home-brew versions of source-code management tools? To me, it comes down to the following facts:
- GitHub's free plan is more than enough for individual users
- GitHub ties very well with the most capable source code management software "git" that is open-source
- GitHub has a very strong track record, earning people's trust on the way, i.e. "it just works"

As someone that builds and tests software for a living, I look at the success story of git and GitHub in awe, "I want this for my software too" I am thinking. The first two facts are self-explanatory, and I would argue that the third fact is by far the most difficult one to achieve. How on earth do you earn trust with your software?
